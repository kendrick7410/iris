## 1. Scope and Purpose of the Economic Outlook Register

Cefic's economic publications serve a different purpose from its news and position content. Where news articles **react** and position papers **advocate**, the economic publications **describe**, **quantify**, and **benchmark**. They are the evidence base the rest of Cefic's communications draw from.

The register is defined by three characteristics:

- **Descriptive-analytical**, not advocacy-driven. Conclusions are allowed to sit implicit in the numbers.
- **Third-person institutional**. No "we" or "our" except in chapter titles like *"Our contribution to EU industry"*. The analyst speaks through the data, not as a personality.
- **Benchmark-heavy**. Every figure is placed against at least one reference frame: year-on-year, pre-crisis average, regional peer (US, China), historical baseline, or sub-sector comparison.

The implicit contract with the reader is: *"These are the facts, measured consistently from public sources. The conclusions are yours to draw — and because we have drawn them transparently from auditable data, they carry weight elsewhere in Cefic's communications."*

## 3. Authorial Voice and Institutional Author

### 3.1 The voice: Cefic's Economics team

Economic content is authored by Cefic's economics team, led by the **Chief Economist**. As of the analysed period:

- **Dr Moncef Hadhri** — Chief Economist, `mha@cefic.be`, direct line `+32 479 79 66 99`
- Person-of-contact for both Facts & Figures and Chemical Trends

This matters editorially: articles referring to economic content (e.g. in press releases announcing a report) should attribute findings to **Cefic analysis** or **the Chief Economist**, not to policy executives like the Director General.

### 3.2 Voice characteristics of this register

- **Neutral third-person.** *"The European chemical industry represents around 635 billion euro in turnover."* Not "we," not "our sector" in body text.
- **Past or present tense**, factual. Future tense used only when reporting projections from named sources.
- **Analytical verbs, not advocacy verbs.** Uses *indicates, reflects, underlines, reveals, shows, points to, highlights* — rarely *calls for, urges, demands*.
- **Hedged forward-looking statements.** *"The gap… is projected to remain substantial between 2025 to 2030."* Projection, not prescription.
- **Attribution to data, not to people.** *"Data analysis shows that…"* rather than *"Cefic says…"*

### 3.3 What this register avoids

The economic outlook register avoids the advocacy signature moves that dominate news and position papers:

- ❌ The welcome–yet pivot
- ❌ *"We call on EU leaders to…"*
- ❌ *"Industry is sending a clear signal…"*
- ❌ Executive quotes in body text
- ❌ First-person plural ("we," "our") outside chapter titles
- ❌ The strategic triad slogan ("sustainable, competitive and resilient")

These appear in the **news article announcing** an economic report, but not inside the report or on the chapter pages themselves.

## 4. The Economic Narrative Arc

Across all six F&F chapters and every Chemical Trends issue analysed, the same narrative arc recurs. It is the **economic backbone** of the Cefic story, told through data:

### 4.1 The four movements

1. **Europe's scale and role** — The European chemical industry is large (€635 bn turnover, 1.2 million jobs, ~13% of world production), deeply integrated into Europe's industrial fabric, and essential to manufacturing value chains (automotive, construction, electronics, pharmaceuticals, healthcare).

2. **China's ascent and Europe's erosion** — The global market has shifted profoundly. China's share rose from 24% to 46% between 2009 and 2024. Europe's fell from 21% to 13% over the same period. The US is roughly stable. This is the single biggest structural story.

3. **The competitiveness squeeze** — Europe faces weak domestic demand, persistently low capacity utilisation (74.0%, around 9.5% below the 2014–2019 pre-crisis average), and a structurally uncompetitive energy price environment (European gas prices three times higher than in the US). Closures are accelerating, investment is shifting elsewhere, and the sector is contracting.

4. **Europe's retained strengths and efficiency story** — Despite the squeeze, the industry has achieved a 40% reduction in specific energy consumption since 1990, remains a net exporter (especially in specialty chemicals), maintains the world's third-largest R&I spend (€10.4 bn in 2024), and retains a dense network of roughly 31,000 companies (97% SMEs).

Every chapter of F&F and every Chemical Trends issue tells one local version of this arc. A new economic piece in this register should sit inside it.

## 5. Audience of Economic Publications

The economic publications serve a slightly different audience than Cefic's news content:

| Primary | Secondary |
|---|---|
| Member company strategy, economics and public affairs teams (for use in their own decks and filings) | Trade press (ICIS, Chemical Week, Platts, ENDS Europe) |
| Financial analysts covering European chemicals (equity and credit) | General press covering industry / EU policy |
| EU institutions' economic units (DG ECFIN, DG GROW, ECB economists) | Academics, think-tanks, consultancies (McKinsey, Roland Berger, S&P Global) |
| Member State industry ministries | Investment community (venture, private equity, LP-level) |

The implication for writing: **analytical precision over rhetorical punch**. Readers are numerate, comparison-literate, and will cross-check against Eurostat. A missing "Source: Eurostat" line damages credibility more than an extra adjective.

### 6.2 Chapter intro paragraph patterns

Three distinct opening patterns are used across the six chapters:

**Pattern A — Anchor fact + context** (used by Profile, Energy, Capital & R&I)
> *"The European chemical industry represents around 635 billion euro in turnover and is an essential element of most everyday products. The EU27 chemical industry, encompassing base, specialty, and consumer chemicals, is vital to the European economy, supplying diverse sectors and employing 1.2 million people."*

**Pattern B — Principle + numerical narrative** (used by Trade Development)
> *"The global trade of chemicals benefits all partners and citizens by stimulating competition, innovation, and production efficiency. The EU27 chemical industry, a key player in the global market, has historically enjoyed a significant trade surplus, averaging €43 bn in the past decade (2011-2021). In the past years, EU27 chemicals surplus has experienced dramatic fluctuations…"*

**Pattern C — Indicator + diagnosis + drivers** (used by Growth & Competitiveness)
> *"The competitiveness of the European chemical sector remains well below pre-crisis levels (2014-2019 average) driven by weak demand and uncompetitive energy prices. This is particularly an issue for high energy consuming, commodity products and petrochemicals, where China holds a competitive edge due to large-scale production and low production costs."*

### 6.3 Intro length and information density

Chapter intros are **two to four paragraphs**, typically **150–300 words**. They:

- Open with a defining fact or principle
- Introduce the main indicator the chapter will detail
- Benchmark the sector against at least one of: pre-crisis (2014–2019), peer regions (US, China), long-run trend, sub-sectors
- Acknowledge a challenge without calling for action (that is left to news and position papers)
- Close on a forward-looking hedged statement or a transition to the charts below

### 6.4 Chapter intros observed — the full set

For reference, the exact opening sentences of each chapter intro in the 2025 edition:

- **Profile:** *"The European chemical industry represents around 635 billion euro in turnover and is an essential element of most everyday products."*
- **Trade Development:** *"The global trade of chemicals benefits all partners and citizens by stimulating competition, innovation, and production efficiency."*

### 7.2 The "descriptive findings as headings" convention

This is the single most distinctive feature of the Chemical Trends PDF and worth highlighting.

Section headings in the PDF are **not topics** (e.g. *"Trade"*, *"Output"*). They are **complete declarative findings**. Examples from the Q3 2025 report:

- *"EU27 chemicals business confidence remains weak in 2025 (Jan-Aug)"*
- *"EU27 chemicals business sentiment deteriorated in 2025 (Jan-Aug)"*
- *"EU27 chemical demand: First nine months of 2025 disappoint"*
- *"Weak demand continues to weigh on EU27 chemicals sales"*
- *"January to September 2025: EU27 chemicals production 2.5% below 2024 levels"*
- *"January to August 2025: EU27 chemical exports value below 2024 levels"*
- *"January to August 2025: EU27 chemical exports volume above 2024 levels"*
- *"January to August 2025: EU27 chemical imports value 2.6% above 2024 levels"*
- *"January to August 2025: EU27 chemical imports volume 5.2% above 2024 levels"*
- *"January to August 2025: EU27 chemical trade surplus in value below 2024 levels"*
- *"January to August 2025: EU27 trade deficit in volume above 2024 levels"*

Each heading tells the reader the finding **before** they read the paragraph or see the chart. A skim-reader should be able to absorb the report just from the headings. This is the single most replicable principle for anyone writing in this register.

| Aspect | Facts & Figures (web chapter) | Chemical Trends (PDF) |

## 8. The Opening Paragraph Formula

The opening paragraph of any economic piece in this register follows a remarkably consistent formula.

### 8.1 The 4-sentence formula (F&F chapter intros)

```
Sentence 1: [Anchor fact establishing scale, value, or role — with a headline number]
Sentence 2: [Structural role or composition — what the sector is made of / who it serves]
Sentence 3: [Benchmark comparison — vs pre-crisis, vs competitor region, vs long-run]
Sentence 4: [Current challenge or diagnostic note — observational, not prescriptive]
```

Worked example (the Facts & Figures 2025 landing page intro, decomposed):

1. *"With a turnover of €635 billion and 1.2 million people employed, supplying essential materials to industries ranging from automotive to healthcare, the European chemical industry remains a pillar of Europe's economy and daily life."* → **anchor fact**
2. *"Yet, the landscape is shifting: Europe's share of the global chemical market has dropped to 13%, while China now leads with 46% of global sales."* → **benchmark comparison**
3. *"Energy remains a critical factor, as European gas prices are still three times higher than in the US, impacting competitiveness."* → **structural diagnostic**
4. *"Nevertheless, the industry has improved its energy efficiency by 40% since 1990."* → **counter-balancing strength**

### 8.2 The 6-bullet formula (Chemical Trends summary box)

```
Theme sentence: [e.g. "EU27 chemical business is struggling"]
• Bullet 1: [Current situation — dense sentence naming the main headwinds]
• Bullet 2: [Competitive pressure — usually China-related]
• Bullet 3: [Structural driver — energy, regulation, openness]
• Bullet 4: [Forward indicator — investment, closures, deindustrialisation]
```

The Q3 2025 summary is a model example:

> **EU27 chemical business is struggling**
> • The current situation in the chemical industry is worrying. Chemical companies are facing a very difficult global economic context. Energy costs in Europe remain among the highest in the world, and demand is still disappointing.
> • The sector continues to face fierce competition, particularly from China. Foreign trade, which has always been a driving force of the European economy, is not significantly improving.
> • The energy prices remain a core concern of the sector. In addition, the recipes that worked in the past seem to have reached their limits: Europe is suffering from being an open market and from its unmatched level of regulation, to the point that Europe has now entered a critical phase. The European chemical sector is a reflection of those patterns, struggling to export more to the international market and facing a significant wave of imports.
> • Uncertainty continues to penalise investment, and forecasts for 2025-2026 are poor. The number of closures in the chemical sector is leading to deindustrialisation, to the benefit of countries offering a cost advantage.

### 9.3 Rules for writing good H2 takeaways

A well-written H2 takeaway in this register meets six criteria:

1. **It is a complete sentence**, not a noun phrase. ✅ *"Weak demand continues to weigh on EU27 chemical production"*. ❌ *"Weak demand"*.
2. **It states the finding**, not the topic. ✅ *"EU27 market share of capital spending dropped significantly"*. ❌ *"EU27 market share of capital spending"*.
3. **It includes a number when available**: *"EU27 chemical capacity utilisation remains steadily low (74%)"*, *"31,000 companies are operating…"*, *"Chemicals are a key contributor to EU27's trade balance (€47 bn)"*.
4. **It references the benchmark when relevant**: *"vs 2023"*, *"compared to the past decade"*, *"since 1990"*, *"2004 vs 2024"*.
5. **It is descriptive, not prescriptive**. ✅ *"Employment in Europe faces increasing competition from Asia, particularly China and India"*. ❌ *"Europe must invest more in employment"*.
6. **It uses plain English, not technical jargon**. The H3 that follows is where the technical precision lives.

### 9.4 Rules for H3 chart titles

H3 chart titles are the **technical twin** of the H2. Six conventions:

1. **Start with the geography**: *"EU27 …"* / *"Extra-EU27 …"* / *"World …"*
2. **Name the indicator precisely**: *"chemical sales"*, *"capacity utilisation rate"*, *"trade flows"*, *"capital spending"*
3. **Include the unit in parentheses**: *"(€ billion)"*, *"(%)"*, *"(thousand FTE)"*, *"(€/megawatt hours)"*, *"(Index, 2009=100)"*
4. **Specify the time window**: *"(2024)"*, *"(2008-2025*)"*, *"(2014 vs 2024)"*, *"(Jan-Aug 2025)"*
5. **Flag estimates or sub-categories with asterisks**: *"(2008-2025\*)"* where \* means estimated / latest available
6. **Match the data file label**: the chart title should correspond to the downloadable Excel sheet name

## 10. The Economic Vocabulary (Specific to This Register)

Many of the phrases below overlap with Cefic's broader lexicon, but the economic outlook register uses them with **specific analytical precision**.

### 10.1 Indicator vocabulary

| Concept | Preferred term |
|---|---|
| Industry size by revenue | *turnover* (not "revenues" or "top line") |
| Industry size by volume | *output*, *production volume*, *production volumes* |
| Price measure | *producer prices*, *chemical prices*, *prices in value* |
| Demand proxy | *domestic sales*, *consumption* |
| Trade in $ terms | *trade in value* |
| Trade in physical terms | *trade in volume*, *in million tons / mt* |
| Production efficiency | *capacity utilisation rate*, *capacity utilisation* |
| Investment spending | *capital spending*, *capital investment* (used interchangeably) |
| R&D spending | *R&I spending* (Research & Innovation — Cefic uses R&I, not R&D) |
| Employment | *direct jobs*, *direct employees*, *employment*, sometimes *FTE* (full-time equivalents) |
| Labour productivity | *labour productivity* (British spelling; "labor" appears in chart titles where US spelling has crept in) |
| Economic climate | *business confidence*, *business sentiment*, *business climate* |

### 10.2 Trajectory vocabulary

Cefic's economics team uses a carefully calibrated set of verbs and adverbs to describe directional change. These are ordered roughly from strongest to weakest:

**Strong decline:** surged downward, plummeted, dropped sharply, fell by X%, declined significantly, deteriorated sharply, dropped by X percentage points

**Moderate decline:** decreased, fell, declined, dropped, lost ground, lost X percentage points, weakened, slowed

**Soft decline / stagnation:** remained below, stayed below, edged down, slightly decreased, barely increased, not significantly improved

**Neutral:** remained at the same level, held steady, continued at, stabilised around

**Soft growth:** edged up, slightly increased, marginally improved, modestly improved, saw a modest improvement

**Moderate growth:** grew, rose, increased, climbed, gained, expanded

**Strong growth:** surged, soared, rose sharply, grew dramatically, expanded substantially, reached record levels, continues to rise

### 10.3 Hedging vocabulary

Economic prose hedges constantly because projections are never certain. Stock phrases:

- *"is projected to…"*
- *"is expected to…"*
- *"trends suggest…"*
- *"forecasts for 2025–2026 are poor"*
- *"business expectations for most downstream users are still not encouraging"*
- *"data analysis shows that…"*
- *"recent data show…"*
- *"the data suggests…"*

### 10.4 Comparative vocabulary (the heart of this register)

- *"above pre-crisis levels (2014–2019)"*
- *"below pre-crisis levels (2014–2019)"*
- *"compared to the same period in [year]"*
- *"year-over-year" / "y-o-y"*
- *"in value" / "in volume"*
- *"compared to [region]"*
- *"compared to [sub-sector]"*
- *"relative to the long-term average"*
- *"relative to EU manufacturing average"*

### 10.5 Structural narrative vocabulary

- *"core concern"* — reserved for persistent, structural issues (energy, capacity utilisation)
- *"key concern"* — used once per report; weighty
- *"structural" / "structurally"* — as in *"Electricity costs in Europe continue to be structurally high"*
- *"fragmented Europe"*, *"different across Europe"* — when describing heterogeneous country results
- *"pioneering role"* — positive, usually reserved for energy efficiency
- *"pivotal role"*, *"critical" (adjective)*, *"vital to"*, *"integral part of"*
- *"fierce competition"* — reserved specifically for China
- *"competitive edge"* — reserved for describing what other regions have
- *"competitive disadvantage"* — reserved for describing Europe's position
- *"lost ground"* — used for European share erosion

### 10.6 Words that signal the arc

| Arc element | Signal words |
|---|---|
| Europe's scale | *pillar*, *essential*, *vital*, *integral part*, *deeply woven*, *cornerstone*, *key player*, *leading* |
| China's ascent | *leads*, *dominance*, *overtakes*, *continues to rise*, *continues to lead*, *contributing to about X%*, *driving the majority* |
| Europe's erosion | *dropped*, *lost ground*, *declined*, *shrunk*, *eroded*, *contracted*, *fell sharply*, *weakened* |
| Europe's strengths | *pioneering*, *net exporter*, *world's third largest*, *still committed*, *recognises the importance of*, *dense network*, *high-value* |
| Structural drivers | *energy-intensive*, *structurally high*, *uncompetitive*, *unmatched level of regulation*, *open market*, *exposed to high risks* |
| Forward-looking | *projected to*, *expected to*, *forecasts for [year] are poor*, *uncertainty continues to*, *does not bode well* |

## 11. Comparative Benchmarks and Reference Frames

Every economic claim in this register is measured against a benchmark. Five benchmarks recur.

### 11.1 The five canonical benchmarks

1. **Pre-crisis baseline: 2014–2019 average.** The most important benchmark in the register. *"The competitiveness of the EU27 chemical sector remains far below pre-crisis levels (2014-2019 average)."*

2. **Year-on-year: [current year] vs [previous year].** Standard for quarterly and annual updates. *"EU27 chemical sales slightly decreased in 2024 vs 2023."*

3. **Decade-over-decade: 2014 vs 2024 / 2004 vs 2024 / 2009 vs 2024.** Used for structural shifts. *"Europe's market share on the global chemical market has declined by 8 percentage points over the past 15 years, from 21% to 13% between 2009 and 2024."*

4. **Regional peer: Europe vs US / Europe vs China / Europe vs Asia.** Used especially for prices, employment, productivity. *"European gas prices were three times higher than in the US."*

5. **Sub-sector: Chemicals vs total manufacturing / Specialty vs Petrochemicals / Upstream vs Downstream.** Used for internal sector analysis. *"EU27 chemical industry itself reported an output decrease of 2.5%, far below the overall EU27 manufacturing average."*

### 11.2 The 2014–2019 pre-crisis frame in detail

The 2014–2019 average is Cefic's preferred baseline because it represents **the last stable, non-disrupted period** for European chemicals — before COVID-19, the 2021–2022 energy crisis, and the structural shift of recent years. When a writer references it, they are signalling: *"measured against the last normal."*

Observed usages:

- *"well below pre-crisis levels (2014-2019 average)"*
- *"9.5% below pre-crisis capacity (2014–2019)"*
- *"10% below the pre-crisis levels of 2014 to 2019"*
- *"slightly above pre-crisis levels (2014-2019)"*
- *"35% above the pre-crisis levels (2014-2019)"*
- *"22% above the pre-crisis levels (2014-2019)"*

Never write *"before the crisis"* or *"pre-pandemic"* — **"pre-crisis levels (2014-2019)"** is the house form.

### 11.3 When to use each benchmark

| If you are writing about… | Use benchmark… |
|---|---|
| Short-term sector health in a quarterly | Year-on-year + pre-crisis |
| Structural market shift | Decade-over-decade (2009 / 2014 / 2004 vs 2024) |
| Prices or competitiveness | Regional peer (US, China) |
| Sub-sector dynamics | Sub-sector comparison + sector total |
| Long-run efficiency gains | Index from 1990 base (e.g. *"since 1990"*, *"since 1991"*) |

## 12. Quantification Conventions

The register is **number-dense**. Every second sentence carries a figure. The conventions below are enforced rigorously.

### 12.1 Currency

| Form | Use |
|---|---|
| **€635 billion** | Full form, especially in leading sentences and headlines |
| **€47 bn** | Short form ("bn" lowercase, no period), used in running prose and tables |
| **€10.4 bn** | Decimal + bn in tight sections |
| **€7.3 bn** | Decimal + bn |
| **€3.2 billion** | Decimal + "billion" in lead or summary contexts |
| **around 635 billion euro** | Occasional alternative form, "euro" not capitalised |
| **€/megawatt hours** | In chart titles and price-per-unit contexts |

Observed: *"€43 bn"*, *"€47.3 bn"*, *"€28.4 bn"*, *"€29.3 bn"*, *"€10.4 bn"*, *"€35.1 bn"*, *"€148.6 bn"*, *"€22.2 bn"*, *"€7.3 bn"*.

### 12.2 Percentages

- *"9%"*, *"13%"*, *"46%"*, *"74.0%"*, *"2.5%"*, *"40%"* — no space between number and %
- *"9.5% below"*, *"2.3% above"* — percentage change
- *"by 8 percentage points"* — use "percentage points" (not %) when comparing two percentage values
- *"x %"* with space is **not** used

### 12.3 Multiples

- *"three times higher"* — preferred form
- *"3.6 times higher"* — with decimal
- *"5 times more"* — for multiples of a count
- *"six-fold"* — used only in news; in economic prose prefer the numeric form

### 12.4 Million, thousand, and "about"

- *"1.2 million jobs"*
- *"31,000 companies"*
- *"30,264 SMEs"* — use full numbers (with commas as thousand separator) for precise figures
- *"around 2.6 million people"*
- *"approximately 89,000 indirect jobs"*
- *"about 25 to 50%"* — approximation with range
- *"up to 47%"* — cap

### 12.5 Tonnes and volumes

- *"61.3 million tons"* — full form
- *"62.8 million tons"*
- *"10.1 million tons"*
- *"5.6 mt"*, *"4.1 mt"*, *"4.0 mt"*, *"252 mt"* — compact form in running prose (lowercase mt, no space)
- **Note:** this differs from the news register's **Mt** (capital M, meaning megatonnes). In the Chemical Trends PDF, *mt* is used in-line for *million tons* as a tight abbreviation. Be consistent within a document: pick one convention.

### 12.6 Rounding conventions

- Headlines and leads: rounded figures — *"around 635 billion euro"*, *"1.2 million jobs"*
- Sub-sector breakdowns: one decimal precision — *"2.3%"*, *"2.5%"*, *"3.4%"*
- Country breakdowns: one decimal — *"-8.5"*, *"+1.9"*, *"-5.3"* (confidence index changes)
- Market shares: integer percentages — *"13%"*, *"46%"*, *"21%"*, *"24%"*

### 12.7 Change expressions

| Type | Preferred |
|---|---|
| Absolute change € | *"down by €6.6 bn compared to 2024"*, *"up by €3.2 billion compared to 2024"* |
| Absolute change volume | *"up by 3.4 million tons"*, *"down from 62.8 million tons in 2024"* |
| Percentage change | *"decreased by 2.3%"*, *"increased by 2.6%"*, *"fell by 32% since 1990"* |
| Percentage-point change | *"by 8 percentage points"* |
| Index change | *"-8.5"*, *"+1.9"* (confidence index) |

## 13. Time References and Periodisation

### 13.1 Date format

- **"3 March 2026"** — standard day-month-year, no commas
- **"Q4 2025"**, **"Q1 2026"** — quarter format with space
- **"2022–2025"**, **"2014–2019"** — en dash, no spaces, full years
- **"2014 vs 2024"** — "vs" lowercase for comparative periods
- **"Jan-Aug 2025"**, **"Jan-Sep 2025"** — three-letter months, hyphen, no space, year at end
- **"January to September 2025"** — full-word form in section headings
- **"the first eight months of 2025"**, **"the first nine months of 2025"** — narrative form
- **"y-o-y"** — year-over-year, lowercase with hyphens, used in chart titles
- **"year-to-date"**, **"year-on-year"** — full English forms in prose
- **"since 1990"** — full year
- **"since Q2 2022"** — quarter-anchored

### 13.2 The "since March 2022" anchor

A recurring time-anchor across both publications is **March 2022** — the start of the energy crisis triggered by Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Cefic's economic prose treats this as a break point:

- *"Since March 2022, the EU27 chemicals business environment has been facing a limited demand…"*
- *"The EU27 chemicals business environment has been facing a limited demand since March 2022"*
- *"It has consistently stayed below the EU27's long-term average since Q2 2022"*

Use this anchor when framing current conditions as structurally distinct from the pre-2022 period.

### 13.3 Publication lag conventions

Both publications follow Eurostat's data lag:

- Annual F&F 2025 edition: published December 2025, with sales data through 2024, trade through 2024, investment through 2024, energy through 2023
- Quarterly Chemical Trends Q3 2025: published 28 November 2025, cover-dated October 2025, with data through August–September 2025 depending on indicator

When writing, be explicit about the data window, not just the publication date. *"Chemical sales in value decreased by 2.3% in the first eight months of 2025"* tells the reader exactly what "2025" means.

## 14. Geographic Hierarchy

### 14.1 The preferred regional label: EU27

Cefic's economics team writes consistently about the **EU27** — the 27 current Member States of the European Union.

- *"EU27"* — no space, no hyphen, ever
- **Never** *"EU-27"*, *"EU 27"*, *"the 27"*
- **Never** *"European Union"* alone when a specific statistical perimeter is meant — always *"EU27"*
- *"Extra-EU27"* — trade flows with countries outside the EU27 (also *"Extra-EU"* informally)
- *"Intra-EU"* — trade flows within the EU27

### 14.2 Country name conventions

Observed forms:

- Germany, France, Italy, the Netherlands, Spain, Belgium, Poland, Austria, Ireland, Finland — **full names**
- US and USA both appear — the register tolerates both, but *"the US"* is more common in prose, *"USA"* in chart titles
- UK, Türkiye — full abbreviation / endonym
- China — spelled out
- Never use codes like DE, FR, IT in body prose (only in chart legends/maps)

### 14.3 Country rankings

When listing countries by any indicator, Cefic's economics team tends to:

- List them in descending order by value
- Include the percentage in parentheses
- Use Oxford-comma-free lists: *"Germany (32% of sales of the chemical sector in Europe), France, (15%), Italy (9%), the Netherlands (9%), Spain (8%), and Belgium (6%)."* (Note the stray comma after "France" — consistent quirks in the live copy.)

### 14.4 Regional groupings beyond the EU27

- *"Asia"* — used especially for employment competition: *"increasing competition from Asia, particularly China and India"*
- *"third countries"* — used in trade / policy context for non-EU countries: *"the competitiveness gap with third countries widened"*
- *"other regions"* — generic comparison

## 15. Sourcing Discipline

### 15.1 The primary source: Eurostat

Chemical Trends data is **entirely Eurostat-based**. Every chart in the Q3 2025 PDF carries a source line of the form:

```
Source: Cefic analysis based on Eurostat data (2025)
Source: Cefic analysis based on Eurostat data, *Jan-Sep 2025
Source: Cefic analysis based on Eurostat data (2025, NACE code 20)
```

Three observations about this convention:

1. **"Cefic analysis based on Eurostat data"** — not *"Source: Eurostat"* alone. Cefic adds interpretive value and flags that.
2. **Year in parentheses** — confirms the data vintage.
3. **NACE code 20** — the statistical classification for *"Manufacture of chemicals and chemical products"*. Cefic includes this on trade-data charts as a precision signal.

### 15.2 Facts & Figures sources

F&F draws on a broader set. From the disclaimer:

> *"Figures within Cefic's Facts & Figures publications are compiled and updated on a regular basis from public statistical sources (Eurostat, EU Commission, European Environmental Agency, …)."*

Named sources that may appear in F&F charts or prose:

- Eurostat
- European Commission (for business and consumer surveys, economic forecasts)
- European Environmental Agency (for energy and emissions indicators)
- National statistical offices (implicit, via Eurostat aggregation)
- OECD (occasionally for labour productivity comparisons)
- ECHA (for regulatory-activity figures)
- Company and trade association data (for chemical industry specific indicators not in Eurostat)

### 15.3 The sourcing protocol

1. **Primary claim → inline source attribution** where possible
2. **Chart → source line directly beneath the chart**, formatted as above
3. **Structural claims (sector size, role)** → sourced in the intro disclaimer, not always re-cited inline
4. **Projections → attributed to the forecasting body** ("the European Commission survey data", "data analysis shows", "forecasts for 2025–2026")
5. **Never**: unattributed "studies show" or "experts say"

### 15.4 NACE code 20

For clarity when writing about the European chemical industry statistically:

- **NACE code 20** covers *"Manufacture of chemicals and chemical products"*
- This is the statistical definition Cefic uses throughout
- It excludes pharmaceuticals (NACE 21) — pharmaceuticals are treated as a separate but related sector
- When quoting *"chemical"* figures from Cefic, NACE 20 is the implicit scope

## 16. The Six Economic Pillars of Facts & Figures

Each F&F chapter focuses on one economic pillar. The six pillars together constitute Cefic's canonical view of the European chemical industry.

### 16.1 Pillar 1: Profile (the *what and where*)

**Focus:** Sector structure, end markets, country distribution, global market share.

**Signature indicators:** EU27 chemicals sales, sales by sub-sector (petrochemicals, specialty, consumer, polymers, basic inorganics), sales by end market (automotive, construction, agriculture, etc.), global share evolution, top producing countries.

**Signature arc:** *"Europe is large and foundational, but China has overtaken it and Europe's share has halved over 15 years."*

### 16.2 Pillar 2: Trade Development

**Focus:** Extra-EU27 trade flows — exports, imports, balance — by sub-sector and by partner country.

**Signature indicators:** Chemical trade balance (€47 bn in 2024), export destinations (US first), import sources (China first), evolution vs 2004, share of world chemical exports.

**Signature arc:** *"Europe remains a net exporter thanks to specialty chemicals, but upstream and petrochemicals are now net importers, and China is gaining share."*

### 16.3 Pillar 3: Growth and Competitiveness

**Focus:** Short-term economic performance — output, capacity utilisation, demand, employment, productivity.

**Signature indicators:** Chemical output index, capacity utilisation (74.0% — the single most-cited indicator), production growth by country, chemical employment by region, labour productivity.

**Signature arc:** *"Europe operates well below pre-crisis capacity, demand is weak, and Asia is gaining on employment and productivity."*

### 16.4 Pillar 4: Our contribution to EU industry

**Focus:** The role chemicals play in the broader European manufacturing fabric.

**Signature indicators:** Number of companies (31,000), SME share (97% of companies), manufacturing added value rank, investment rank (3rd), employment rank (7th), labour costs (3rd), contribution to manufacturing trade balance.

**Signature arc:** *"Chemicals are a top-five manufacturing sector across almost every cut — a structural backbone."*

### 16.5 Pillar 5: Energy Consumption

**Focus:** Energy intensity, sources of energy, natural gas price gap, efficiency trajectory.

**Signature indicators:** Natural gas prices Europe vs USA (3x gap), total energy consumption by source (gas + electricity = two-thirds), energy consumption down 32% since 1990, specific energy consumption down 40% since 1991.

**Signature arc:** *"Chemicals are energy-intensive, European gas prices are structurally 3x the US level, but the sector is a pioneer in energy efficiency."*

### 16.6 Pillar 6: Capital & R&I Spending

**Focus:** Investment in physical capacity and in research & innovation.

**Signature indicators:** EU27 capital spending (€28.4 bn in 2024), R&I spending (€10.4 bn in 2024), global market share of both, evolution since 2009, regional comparison.

**Signature arc:** *"EU27 remains a top-three R&I investor but has lost global capital spending share, while China now drives almost half of global chemicals investment."*

## 18. Grammatical Register: How Economic Prose Differs from News

### 18.1 Ten differences between economic prose and news/position prose

| Feature | News / Position | Economic Outlook |
|---|---|---|
| Voice | Plural "we" / "industry" | Third-person institutional ("the industry", "the sector", "EU27 chemical industry") |
| Executive presence | Named quotes from DG, DDG, President | No named executives; authorship via Chief Economist in metadata only |
| Quotes | One per article; pull-quoted | None in body; occasional italicised summary quotes at most |
| Verbs | *calls for, urges, welcomes, stresses* | *shows, reveals, reflects, indicates, suggests, reports, amounts to* |
| Hedging | Hedging is rare | Hedging is constant — *projected to, expected to, is likely to* |
| Sentence type | Mostly active, short, punchy | Mixed; longer sentences with multiple subordinate clauses tolerated for precision |
| Numbers | Rounded, dramatic — *six-fold, 37 Mt, 20,000 jobs* | Precise — *2.3%, €148.6 bn, 74.0%, 61.3 million tons* |
| Context per number | Implicit ("we all know what this means") | Explicit — year, region, sub-sector, comparison base |
| Closing beat | Forward-looking call to action | Forward-looking hedged projection |
| Use of H2/H3 | Almost never (one ## Background) | Constantly — every chart has an H2 takeaway and H3 chart title |

### 18.2 British English — same as news

Economic publications use **British English** throughout:

- *organisation, utilisation, capacity utilisation, decarbonisation, specialisation, analyse, labour*

**Exception:** a handful of chart titles show drift toward American spellings (*"Chemical labor productivity"* appears once on the Growth & Competitiveness chapter alongside *"Chemical labour productivity"*). When editing, prefer British; when consistency in a chart data-file requires otherwise, note the drift.

### 18.3 Hyphenation of compound modifiers

Economic prose is disciplined on hyphens:

- *high-quality*, *low-carbon*, *pre-crisis*, *long-term*, *year-on-year*, *hard-to-abate*, *evidence-based*, *high-value*, *large-scale*, *energy-intensive*
- Post-nominal forms drop the hyphen: *"the crisis was pre-2020"* vs *"pre-crisis levels"*

### 18.4 Sentence-level conventions

- **Average sentence length: 22–28 words** (longer than news — closer to academic or analyst-report register)
- **Paragraph length: 3–5 sentences**
- **Complex sentences with subordinate clauses are welcome** when precision demands — *"The EU27 chemicals trade surplus amounted to €25.0 bn in the first eight months of 2025, down by €6.6 bn compared to 2024."*
- **No rhetorical questions**
- **No exclamation marks** (none, anywhere, in the economic register — the Chemical Trends news article headline *"Cefic's Chemical Trends Report Q4 2025 is out!"* is the **news** register, not the report itself)

### 18.5 Acronym discipline

- First use: spell out + parenthetical acronym — *"Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs)"*
- **SME** — the standard acronym, widely used without expansion after first use
- **NACE** — statistical classification, used in source lines
- **EU27**, **EU** — universally recognised, no expansion needed
- **R&I** — Research & Innovation, spelled out on first use in longer pieces
- **FTE** — Full-Time Equivalent, spelled out on first use
- **USD** / **€** / **GWh** / **mt** / **MWh** — unit abbreviations, always used with numerals

## 19. The Mandatory Disclaimer

Every Facts & Figures landing page carries a standing disclaimer. It is part of the institutional voice of the register and should be reproduced verbatim on annual editions:

> **Disclaimer:**
>
> *"For the sake of data integrity, figures within Cefic's Facts & Figures publications are compiled and updated on a regular basis from public statistical sources (Eurostat, EU Commission, European Environmental Agency, …). These sources regularly review their previous years' data and at times retroactively amend it. As a result of these updates, the comparison of annual Cefic Facts & Figures editions is not necessarily consistent over time."*

Three editorial implications:

1. **Never claim exact continuity** across F&F editions in citations. If quoting a 2023 edition for a 2024 figure, flag that the figure may have been restated.
2. **Always cite the edition**, not just "Cefic Facts & Figures" — *"Facts & Figures 2025 edition"* is the preferred citation form.
3. **Never imply the data is Cefic's own** — it is *compiled from public sources* with analytical overlay.

The Chemical Trends PDF does not carry a standing disclaimer, but the inline source lines under each chart serve the same function.

## 21. Checklists

### 21.1 Economic-outlook universal checklist

- [ ] Register is third-person institutional (no "we," no "our" except in chapter titles like *"Our contribution to EU industry"*)
- [ ] No executive quotes in body text
- [ ] Every quantitative claim carries a comparison (YoY, pre-crisis, regional, sub-sector)
- [ ] British English spelling (organisation, utilisation, labour, programme)
- [ ] **Cefic** never **CEFIC**
- [ ] **EU27** never "EU-27" or "EU 27"
- [ ] Every chart has a **Source: Cefic analysis based on Eurostat data (YYYY)** line (or equivalent for F&F sources)
- [ ] Currencies use the convention: €X billion (full) or €X bn (compact)
- [ ] Percentages without space: 9%, 13%, 74.0%
- [ ] Year ranges use en dash, no spaces: 2022–2025
- [ ] Dates: D Month YYYY, never abbreviated month
- [ ] Pre-crisis benchmark phrased as *"pre-crisis levels (2014–2019)"*
- [ ] The 1.2 million jobs / €635 bn / 13% of world sales anchor numbers are current for the edition
- [ ] The intro paragraph follows the 4-sentence formula (anchor fact → structural role → benchmark → current diagnostic)
- [ ] No advocacy verbs (*call on, urge, demand*) — only analytical verbs (*shows, indicates, reflects*)

### 21.2 Checklist for a new Facts & Figures chapter

- [ ] Intro is 2–4 paragraphs, 150–300 words
- [ ] Intro ends before any headings, bullets, or lists
- [ ] Each chart has an H2 takeaway statement (complete declarative sentence)
- [ ] Each chart has an H3 technical title (geography + indicator + unit + time)
- [ ] Takeaway ≠ chart title
- [ ] 4–11 chart triplets per chapter
- [ ] Side navigation lists anchor links to every chart in the chapter
- [ ] Person-of-contact card (Chief Economist) is present
- [ ] Chapter is cross-linked to the other five chapters at the foot

### 21.3 Checklist for a new Chemical Trends issue

- [ ] Cover page with *"CHEMICAL TRENDS REPORT"* title and month-year
- [ ] About-Cefic box with the 1.2M jobs / 13% world production anchor sentence
- [ ] Chief Economist contact line with phone and email
- [ ] SUMMARY page with a theme-statement and 4 bullets
- [ ] Report-description box clarifying what the report is and what indicators it covers
- [ ] **IN-DEPTH ANALYSIS** H1 section divider
- [ ] Every indicator section has a descriptive H1 that states the finding, not the topic
- [ ] Every chart carries its own inline source line
- [ ] Both value and volume are reported for trade indicators
- [ ] Sub-sector breakdown (specialty, petrochemicals, consumer, polymers, basic inorganics) is provided where relevant
- [ ] Country breakdown (Germany, France, Italy, Netherlands, Spain, Belgium, Poland) is provided for output and sales indicators
- [ ] NACE code 20 is referenced in source lines for trade data

### 21.4 Checklist for a new H2 takeaway

- [ ] It is a complete sentence
- [ ] It states the finding, not the topic
- [ ] It includes a number where available
- [ ] It references the benchmark when relevant (vs 2023, since 1990, vs US)
- [ ] It is descriptive, not prescriptive
- [ ] It is plain English, not technical jargon
- [ ] It is different from the H3 chart title below it

### 21.5 Checklist for a new Chemical Trends section heading

- [ ] It begins with a time window (e.g. *"January to September 2025:"*, *"in 2025 (Jan-Aug)"*)
- [ ] It names the geography (EU27)
- [ ] It names the specific indicator
- [ ] It carries the comparison (above / below [prior year] levels; percentage change; versus benchmark)
- [ ] It works as a standalone sentence — a reader skimming only the headings should understand the report
